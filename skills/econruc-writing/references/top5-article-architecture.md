# Top5 Economics Article Architecture

Use this guide for abstracts, introductions, results, discussion, conclusion, and contribution framing.

## Top5 logic

Top economics journals usually ask:

1. What economic question is being answered?
2. Why is the question unresolved or mismeasured?
3. What variation, model, or institutional setting makes progress possible?
4. What is the estimated effect or mechanism in economically meaningful units?
5. Why should the result change how economists think about the market, policy, model, or behavior?
6. What are the limits of the evidence?

The paper should not merely say that a topic is important. It should show why the current evidence cannot answer the specific margin and why this design can.

## Abstract architecture

Default abstract:

`Question -> setting/data -> design -> result magnitude -> mechanism/interpretation -> contribution/boundary`

Template:

`We study [question] using [data/setting] and [source of variation]. [Design sentence]. We find that [effect size and unit]. The effect is [heterogeneity/mechanism], consistent with [economic channel]. These results [contribution], while [boundary].`

For Chinese abstract:

`本文考察[问题]。基于[数据/制度背景]，本文利用[识别策略]识别[对象]对[结果变量]的影响。结果表明，[核心结果及经济量级]。进一步分析显示，[机制或异质性]。本文的贡献在于[贡献]，但结论的外部有效性主要限于[边界]。`

## Introduction architecture

Paragraph jobs:

1. Economic stakes: define the welfare, efficiency, distributional, or market-design relevance.
2. Knowledge gap: explain why existing evidence cannot answer the margin.
3. Setting and data: introduce the empirical opportunity.
4. Identification: explain the variation without burying the reader in notation.
5. Findings: give magnitudes and interpretation.
6. Mechanisms and robustness: summarize support for interpretation.
7. Contributions: map to literatures.

Avoid starting with policy slogans. Start with a durable economic problem.

## Results architecture

Strong results paragraphs follow:

`Claim sentence -> design reminder -> estimate and unit -> uncertainty -> interpretation -> next evidence`

Example:

`The reform reduced entry among small firms. In the baseline specification, treated counties experience a 4.2 percent decline in new firm registrations relative to comparison counties (s.e. = 1.1). The magnitude corresponds to roughly one-third of the pre-reform annual growth rate. Event-study estimates show no differential pre-trends, while the decline emerges after implementation.`

## Contribution architecture

Separate contribution types:

- `Substantive`: answer a core question about behavior, markets, firms, households, or policy.
- `Identification`: isolate a margin that prior work could not credibly separate.
- `Data/measurement`: construct or validate a new measure that changes inference.
- `Mechanism`: distinguish between competing channels.
- `External validity`: test whether known mechanisms travel to a new institution or population.

Contribution paragraph template:

`This paper contributes to [literature] in three ways. First, ... Second, ... Third, ...`

Do not use `fills a gap` unless the gap is explicitly defined as a question, not a topic.

## Claim calibration

- `causes`: use only when identification supports causal interpretation.
- `is associated with`: descriptive or correlational.
- `suggests`: mechanism or interpretation evidence.
- `is consistent with`: mechanism or theory alignment.
- `rules out`: only when the test directly excludes an explanation.
- `speaks to`: contribution to broader debate without overclaiming.
