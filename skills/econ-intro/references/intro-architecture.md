# Economics Introduction Architecture

Use these architectures flexibly. The goal is not to force every paper into one template, but to make the reader understand the question, design, finding, and contribution quickly.

## English Top5 / leading field-journal funnel

1. **Economic stakes and object**
   - Start with the economic margin, not a broad social slogan.
   - Good openings often name a tradeoff, allocation problem, incentive, constraint, or welfare-relevant margin.
   - Avoid generic openings such as `X is important for economic development` unless the next sentence makes the precise margin clear.

2. **Unresolved question**
   - State what economists do not yet know.
   - The gap should be about evidence, mechanism, identification, measurement, or theory, not simply `few papers study this setting`.

3. **Why the question is hard**
   - Explain the identification or measurement problem.
   - Examples: endogenous policy adoption, sorting, anticipation, unobserved productivity, equilibrium spillovers, measurement error, selection into treatment, or missing counterfactuals.

4. **Setting, data, and design**
   - Introduce the setting only after it is clear why the setting is useful.
   - Link variation to the research question: `This setting is useful because...`
   - Name the estimator only when it helps the reader understand the design.

5. **Main result**
   - State sign, magnitude, and benchmark.
   - Interpret in economic units: percent of baseline, standard deviations, fiscal cost, productivity, welfare, employment, prices, output, or distributional incidence.

6. **Mechanism, heterogeneity, and robustness preview**
   - Use concise preview language.
   - Mechanism evidence should be described as evidence, not definitive proof, unless separately identified.

7. **Contribution map**
   - Separate contributions:
     - substantive: what we learn about the economic question
     - design/data: why this evidence is credible or newly measurable
     - literature: how it changes or sharpens existing debates

8. **Roadmap**
   - Include only if the target venue or manuscript style expects it.
   - Keep it short; many economics papers can omit a formulaic roadmap.

## Chinese top-journal introduction

Chinese introductions often tolerate more institutional context and policy motivation, but the economics still needs to drive the section.

1. **现实背景与问题意识**
   - 说明制度背景、政策变化或现实矛盾。
   - 避免只写宏观口号，要落到可研究的经济行为或资源配置问题。

2. **理论或经验争议**
   - 说明既有研究为什么尚未回答核心问题。
   - 可以用“已有研究主要从……展开，但对……仍缺乏直接证据”。

3. **识别难点与本文切入**
   - 明确内生性、选择偏误、反事实构造、机制识别或数据测量难点。
   - 让读者看到本文不是换一个样本重复已有研究。

4. **数据、方法与主要发现**
   - 交代数据来源、样本范围、核心变量和识别策略。
   - 主要结论要有数量含义，不能只说“显著促进”。

5. **边际贡献**
   - 建议分三类写：
     - 理论或问题贡献
     - 数据或识别贡献
     - 机制、政策或中国情境贡献

6. **结构安排**
   - 中文期刊常保留文章结构段，但应简洁。

## Bilingual workflow

- First build the argument in English if the target is an international journal.
- Then write the Chinese version as a logic-preserving adaptation.
- Do not translate `identification` mechanically as only `识别`; use `识别策略`, `识别假设`, `识别变异`, or `因果识别` depending on context.
- Translate contribution language by function:
  - `speaks to` -> `回应/对话于`
  - `provides evidence on` -> `为……提供经验证据`
  - `identifies` -> `识别/估计/揭示`, depending on design strength
  - `mechanism` -> `作用机制` or `影响渠道`

## Paragraph length and rhythm

- English: shorter paragraphs, usually 120-220 words, with the first sentence doing real argumentative work.
- Chinese: paragraphs may be longer, but avoid stacking background, literature, and contribution in one paragraph.
- In both languages, do not bury the empirical design after a long literature review.
