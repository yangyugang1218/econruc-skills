# Top5 and Field Journal Framing

## Top5 framing

Top5-style positioning usually needs:

- broad economic question
- clean identification or theoretical insight
- relevance beyond one policy or city
- mechanism or welfare interpretation
- contribution to a named literature

Frame the setting as an empirical opportunity, not as the contribution itself.

## Field-journal framing

Field journals can support more institutional detail and narrower audiences, but still need:

- clear question
- credible method
- contribution to field debate
- transparent limitations

## Chinese economics journal framing

For Chinese journals such as `经济研究`, `管理世界`, `经济学季刊`, `世界经济`, `金融研究`, and strong CSSCI field journals, adapt the framing:

- State the China-specific institutional background clearly, but do not make `China` the whole contribution.
- Connect to an economic mechanism or policy margin that travels beyond the setting.
- Use more explicit policy relevance than Top5 English journals, but keep causal language tied to design.
- Explain data construction and variable definitions more fully when using Chinese administrative or yearbook data.
- For `经济研究`, emphasize theoretical mechanism, identification credibility, and contribution to economics literature.
- For `管理世界`, allow more policy and management implications, but preserve empirical discipline.
- For `经济学季刊`, emphasize clean question, transparent empirical strategy, and relation to Chinese/English literatures.

Chinese contribution template:

`本文可能的边际贡献体现在三个方面：第一，围绕...问题，本文提供了...证据；第二，利用...识别策略/数据，本文能够刻画...；第三，机制分析表明...，从而为...政策讨论提供了经验证据。`

## Field-specific cues

- Labor: workers, firms, wages, employment, search, human capital, discrimination.
- Development: institutions, infrastructure, markets, state capacity, poverty, agriculture.
- Public: taxation, transfers, local public finance, externalities, government behavior.
- IO: market power, entry, pricing, product differentiation, regulation.
- Finance: credit, risk, intermediation, household finance, corporate finance.
- Urban: agglomeration, housing, migration, transportation, local amenities.
- Health: insurance, provider incentives, patient behavior, mortality, morbidity.
- Education: achievement, human capital, school quality, peer effects.
- Environmental: pollution, regulation, climate risk, adaptation, energy.
- Political economy: incentives, accountability, institutions, voting, bureaucracy.

## Language

Use `speaks to`, `contributes to`, `extends`, `provides evidence on`, `distinguishes between`.

Avoid `proves`, `subverts`, `breaks through`, `fills a blank`.
